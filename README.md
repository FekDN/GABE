# Synthesis-of-Functional-Representations-for-Weight-Tensors

## 1. Introduction: The Structural Redundancy Hypothesis

Modern neural networks, especially large language models, contain billions of parameters, which creates substantial challenges for storage, deployment, and execution. This work is based on the hypothesis that the weight tensors of such models are not collections of random, independent numbers. Instead, they are structured signals exhibiting significant **structural redundancy**.

This redundancy is hypothesized to arise from the model architecture itself, in particular from the use of repeated blocks (e.g., 12 or more structurally identical layers in Transformers). These blocks solve similar subproblems, making it reasonable to assume that their weight matrices, while not identical, are generated by the same low-dimensional “law” or “template”.

The original goal was to **replace large weight tensors with compact, parameterized mathematical functions** capable of generating weights on-the-fly inside computational kernels (fused kernels), thereby radically reducing memory requirements.

---

## 2. Methodology: Toward an Operator-Centric Representation

The solution path was iterative and included several approaches, each providing important insights.

### Attempt #1: Direct Functional Approximation (Failure)

The initial idea was to treat weights as a smooth function of their coordinates:  
`W[i, j] = F(i, j)`, and to approximate `F` using a polynomial.

This approach failed. The residual  
`W_residual = W_original - W_polynomial`  
was dense and nearly identical to the original tensor.

**Conclusion:** Weight tensors are not simple smooth functions. Their structure is substantially more complex.

---

### Attempt #2: Statistical Approximation of Operators (Partial Success)

The next idea was to compress not the tensor itself, but its *statistical effect* on an operation. For example, replacing matrix `W` in `X @ W` with a set of its statistical invariants (norm, mean, etc.).

This method achieved extreme compression but was **fundamentally lossy**. It could not guarantee exact reconstruction, which contradicted the core objective.

---

### Breakthrough: Operator-Centric Decomposition via PCA/SVD (Final Method)

The key insight was to stop guessing the basis and instead **extract the optimal basis directly from the data**.

Each weight tensor `W` was treated as a single point in a high-dimensional space. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) / Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) was applied to the entire collection of such points.

This yielded an exact analytical representation:

 $$
 W^{(l)} = \bar{W} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} c_{l,k} \cdot B_k
 $$

(W⁽ˡ⁾ = W̄ + ∑ₖ₌₁ᴷ cₗ,ₖ · Bₖ)

Where:

- `W^(l)` — the weight tensor of layer `l`.
- `\bar{W}` — the **mean operator**, a shared baseline.
- `B_k` — **basis operators** (“eigen-weights”).
- `c_{l,k}` — **scalar coefficients** defining the layer coordinates.

---

### Implementation

1. **Compiler (`EigenBasisExtractor`)**  
   Computes `\bar{W}`, `B_k` (via `torch.linalg.svd`), and coefficients `c_{l,k}`.

2. **Interpreter (`operator_centric_matmul`)**  
   Computes `Y = X @ W` without materializing `W`:

   \[
   Y^{(l)} = X \cdot \bar{W} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} c_{l,k} \cdot (X \cdot B_k)
   \]

## 3. Results: Numerical Evidence on GPT-2 Weights

| Group (Role)        | Shape           | Rank (K) | Approximation MSE | Compression |
|---------------------|-----------------|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **FFN Layer 1**     | `(768, 3072)`   | 4 of 12  | **4.076e-06**     | **2.40×**   |
| **Attention QKV**   | `(768, 2304)`   | 4 of 12  | 1.216e-05         | 2.40×       |
| **Attention Out**  | `(768, 768)`    | 4 of 12  | 1.430e-04         | 2.40×       |
| **FFN Layer 2**     | `(3072, 768)`   | 4 of 12  | **7.133e-03**     | 2.40×       |

### Key Observations

1. **Exactness Verified**  
   Full-rank reconstruction yields MSE ≈ `1e-12`–`1e-15`.

2. **High Compression**  
   Low-rank (`K=4`) achieves 2.4× compression.

3. **Negligible Error**  
   For FFN1: `sqrt(MSE) ≈ 0.002`.

4. **Architectural Insight**  
   Expanding layers are more redundant than compressing layers.

## 4. Detailed Group Analysis: The Story Told by ResNet

By comparing groups of convolutional kernels, we can observe how feature complexity evolves as data propagates through the network.

| Rank | Kernel Group (Shape)        | MSE at K=2 | Compression at K=2 | Interpretation |
|-----:|-----------------------------|------------|--------------------|----------------|
| #1 | Early layers `(64, 64, 3, 3)` | 7.18e-06 | 1.33× | **EXTREMELY REDUNDANT.** Lowest error. Early ResNet layers learn very simple and universal filters (edge, corner, gradient detectors). All layers in this group are effectively small variations of the same template. |
| #2 | Deep layers `(512, 512, 3, 3)` | 3.20e-04 | 7.00× | **HIGHLY REDUNDANT.** Despite being the deepest and most complex layers, they compress remarkably well. This indicates repeated use of complex but shared patterns (e.g., texture or object-part detectors). Approximating 21 such tensors with only 2–4 basis filters is a critical finding. |
| #3 | Middle layers `(256, 256, 3, 3)` | 6.17e-04 | 3.00× | **HIGH REDUNDANCY.** Transitional stage where combinations of basic features become more complex. |
| #4 | 1×1 bottleneck layers | ~2.50e-03 | ~3.33× | **SPECIALIZED.** Error is an order of magnitude higher than for 3×3 convolutions. This is expected: 1×1 convolutions perform channel-wise linear mixing rather than spatial pattern detection. Their function is inherently more layer-specific, analogous to FFN2 in GPT-2. |

## 5. GPT-2: Detailed Group Analysis — From Universals to Specialists

Groups are ranked from most redundant (easiest to compress) to most unique (hardest to compress), using MSE at `K=4`.

| Rank | Layer Group (Role) | MSE at K=4 | Interpretation |
|-----:|--------------------|------------|----------------|
| #1 | **FFN1 (c_fc)** — Feature Expansion | 4.32e-06 | **EXTREMELY REDUNDANT.** Almost all information from 12 layers is captured by only 4 base templates. These are universal, interchangeable feature extractors. |
| #2 | **Attention (c_attn)** — QKV Projection | 1.20e-05 | **HIGHLY REDUNDANT.** The projection into Query, Key, and Value spaces is a shared, universal operation across the model. |
| #3 | **Attention (c_proj)** — Attention Output Assembly | 1.43e-04 | **MODERATELY SPECIALIZED.** Error is an order of magnitude higher, indicating that while the overall structure is shared, each layer aggregates attention outputs in a slightly different way. |
| #4 | **FFN2 (c_proj)** — Feature Compression | 7.13e-03 | **EXTREMELY SPECIALIZED.** Error is three orders of magnitude higher than FFN1. These layers are the most unique in the model, each performing a distinct compression of processed information back into the residual stream. |

## 6. Discussion: From Compression to Intelligent Regularization

The central reinterpretation is that approximation error (`MSE`) may quantify **removed overfitting**, not lost signal.

- **`W_base` (low-rank approximation)** — denoised, generalizable signal.
- **`W_residual`** — filtered noise / overfitting artifacts.

This method constitutes **structural, data-driven regularization**. Unlike L2 or Dropout, it identifies the optimal low-dimensional subspace directly from data and projects weights onto it.

---

## Final Conclusions

- **Can a tensor be replaced by a fused kernel?**  
  Yes. Mathematically equivalent with controllable precision.

- **Can optimal candidates for replacement be identified?**  
  Yes. Via MSE–rank curves per group.

- **Can size be reduced with negligible loss?**  
  Yes. The removed components likely correspond to overfitting and may improve generalization.
